    The availability heuristic is a cognitive bias where people judge the probability or frequency of an event based on how easily examples of it come to mind. If something can be recalled quickly and vividly, we tend to believe it is more common or likely to occur, even if it is statistically rare.

    This heuristic is influenced by factors such as media coverage, personal experience, emotional impact, and recency of exposure. For example, people often overestimate the risk of plane crashes or shark attacks because such events are highly publicized and memorable, while underestimating more common but less dramatic risks like car accidents or heart disease. The availability heuristic demonstrates how our intuitive judgments can be distorted when they rely more on salience than on factual data.
